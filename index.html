Ah, the State of the Union.

Unlike statements from the White House press room, people actually tune in. By the tens of millions, Americans listen to a calculated speech, occasionally interrupted by lopsided standing ovations.

I'm one of those Americans. I watch. I listen. I try and hang on 'til the end but never make it because it's a Hallmark movie where the characters aren't hot. The SOTU is predictable and highly scripted, where every conflict has a clear resolution. So I set out to look at every State of the Union address, from George to Donald, and run a series of language analyses on them.

The first looks at the maturity of their language, or what age groups could understand their speeches. The second measures language that projects excitement, power, and abstraction. The third is all about politicization, i.e. politically provocative language.

Data & words by me, Josh.
1/3 — MATURITY

My fellow Americans of all ages...

A couple hundred years ago, the State of the Union was one powdered wig talking to a room of powdered wigs. More than half the room still believed that only property-owning guys that use words like "sagacity" and "condign" deserve power.

As you'll see immature language increases as America matures. It makes sense that once radio hit the airwaves, the language Presidents used no longer required three degrees and a deep understanding of Voltaire. They spoke to everyone.

There are a two anomalies. First, despite being the first president to speak to a national audience via satellite, Jimmy Carter's language was about as intelligible as George Washington's. Perhaps when addressing congress, Jimmy's soil-tilling roots were subdued.

Second, Chester A. Arthur (1881-1885), nicknamed "The Gentleman Boss," addressed the nation with the most advanced vocabulary of any president. Curiously, his successor Grover Cleveland — the first Democrat to be elected after the Civil War and nicknamed "Grover the Good" — spoke with the least advanced vocabulary of any president. Perhaps this was an attempt to appeal to broader constituencies, or a direct repudiation of The Gentlemen Boss.

AROUSAL, DOMINANCE, & CONCRETENESS
The data below leverages a model that extrapolates and analyzes English words to determine arousal (emotionally "exciting" or intense), dominance (words that suggest power, influence, or control), and concreteness (words that refer to things you can see, touch, or otherwise experience directly with your senses).
2/3 — POWER, EXCITEMENT, & ABSTRACTION

Keep it simple, stupid.

Since Y2K, Presidents deployed crystal clear language (i.e. high "concreteness") at the SOTU. Instead of "mechanized beasts bridging distances with cargo," Trump, Biden, Obama, and GWB say "truck." Low concept. High reality.

Oddly, this wasn't the only stretch in history where concrete language prevailed over abstraction. From the onset of the civil war up through WWI, things were incredibly nonconceptual — time to talk in military, not heady, parlance. Guns are guns. Foes are foes. To. The. Point.

But concreteness dips just after as reform movements and growing globalization set in. To communicate big ideas like international diplomacy and social welfare programs, abstract language was needed.

Words that evoke power and excitement follow a similar trend, with some notable exceptions: likely due to 9/11, George W. Bush's language, infused with overtones of conquest and revenge, ranks ahead of the rest in "dominance" and "arousal." Among the lowest in these categories: Dwight Eisenhower, who had the pleasure of overseeing an America fresh from a WWII victory and entering an era of peace. Or so he said.

3/3 — POLITICIZATION

It gets the people going.

Without Russian interference or burning ballots, The Don tops our third and final category.

Perhaps it was the perfect storm of social media, populist rhetoric, and an inability to read teleprompters. Perhaps it was the reality tv-ication of our politics. Regardless, Trump is an outlier, using language clocked as "toxic" twice the average.

Strangely, the only other presidents that creep toward the top of heavily politicized penthouse of Trump Tower are paires of relatives in the Roosevelts and Bushes. You could make the argument that both Roosevelts faced major crises (Teddy with global power shifts, Franklin with the Depression and WWII), prompting stronger language.

You could say the Bushes similarly dealt with conflicts like the Gulf War (George H.W. Bush) and the War on Terror (Dubya), leading to more charged speeches. But every President had some catastrophe to deal with, right? Gulf War schmulf war!

God bless America and goodnight.

Donald Trump is a president defined (mostly) by his speech. The way he addresses the public carries a tone and attitude that communicates something his words don't. And whatever it is, a narrow majority loves it.

Donald's SOTU ratings are better than Biden's, but still lesser than George W. Bush and Bill Clinton. Don't tell him. So if you do tune in, consider the message beneath the message.
